Motivation: Why Track B, Why Topic B4

The cultural gap that motivated TimeLens — a 200-word statement

In Chinese family life, love is real but rarely spoken. Shaped by cultural restraint and a deep aversion to being a burden, parents and grandparents withhold their stories — about hardship, migration, lost siblings, first jobs — for fear of imposing on children who "are already busy." Children, in turn, hesitate to ask, worried that sentimental questions will sound inappropriate or embarrassing. The result is a quiet generational silence that no current tool addresses. Voice calls demand emotional readiness on both sides; family group chats reward jokes and logistics, not memories; cloud photo albums store images without the stories that made them matter.

We chose Track B (Social Connectivity) because this gap is, fundamentally, a social-connectivity problem rather than a content-creation one. And we chose Topic B4 (Relation between Generations) because the bridge needs to span three generations, not two — grandparents as memory holders, parents as cultural mediators, grandchildren as digital natives who want to listen but don't know how to begin.

Our response, TimeLens, replaces the awkward question with a playful, object-mediated invitation: a tap on a meaningful keepsake, a sensory reveal, a short reply. Reluctance becomes curiosity. Affection arrives without the difficult sentence. This is why playfulness is essential rather than decorative — it gives both generations permission to engage without losing face.

The Gap (Commercial Products)

Four leading products in adjacent spaces — three strengths and three misses each, evaluated against the 3-generation Chinese-family context.

1 · Ancestry.com

Genealogy / Family Tree · Web + Mobile
3 Strengths
  • Authoritative archival depth via census records, civil documents, and DNA matching
  • Multi-decade platform stability — family data survives generations
  • Tree metaphor makes generational structure instantly legible
3 Misses
  • Form-based input — emotional context is reduced to optional notes
  • One-way authoring — elders are researched about, not invited to narrate themselves
  • Euro-American kinship model — multi-generational households and oral memory traditions do not fit
Visit product

2 · StoryWorth

Weekly memoir prompts · Email + Year-end printed book
3 Strengths
  • Weekly emailed prompts remove the "where do I start" paralysis for older users
  • Year-end printed book gives a physical, heirloom-quality artifact
  • Async and dignified — the elder writes alone, with no audience pressure
3 Misses
  • No reply loop — children read but rarely respond inline; effort feels one-way
  • Text-heavy and English-prompt-driven — Chinese grandparents often prefer voice or object anchors
  • Decoupled from keepsakes — the watch, recipe card, and letter stay silent in the drawer
Visit product

3 · WeChat Family Group + Group Album

Universal Chinese family chat · iOS + Android
3 Strengths
  • Already universal across generations — zero onboarding gap, every family member is on it
  • Mixed-modality (voice, sticker, short video, photo) supports varied generational comfort levels
  • Real-time group visibility reinforces shared family awareness
3 Misses
  • Logistics and morning-greeting GIFs flood the chat — meaningful stories scroll away within hours
  • No story scaffolding — a single keepsake photo gets one caption, no follow-up structure
  • Cultural barrier intact — users still face the awkward "Mom, tell me about Grandma" first message
Visit product

4 · Marco Polo

Async video messaging · iOS + Android
3 Strengths
  • One-tap async video — no scheduling across time zones or elder energy levels
  • Voice and face preserve warmth that plain text loses across generations
  • Direct-to-recipient feels intimate without being performative like a public post
3 Misses
  • Still requires self-recording — the exact barrier elders find uncomfortable
  • Ephemeral chronological feed — no concept of "the watch's story" as a returnable artifact
  • Screen-only — household objects stay disconnected from the memories they hold
Visit product
What the gap tells us:

Existing tools optimize for archival depth (Ancestry), solo memoir (StoryWorth), real-time chat (WeChat), or async video (Marco Polo) — none combine tangible-object trigger + multi-sensory reveal + lightweight cross-generation reply. This is the design white-space TimeLens occupies, and the rationale behind our 3 Must-Have Playful Requirements below.

3 Must-Have Playful Requirements

The minimum conditions for the system to feel playful, not merely functional — derived from journey pain points, persona quotes, the 24-sample survey, and the cited literature.

MH-1 · Tangible Trigger

Replace the awkward question with a physical action

The system must let users initiate a memory exchange through physical action (tap an NFC-tagged keepsake), not a verbal or text request.

Cultural pain
In Chinese family culture, asking "Grandpa, tell me a story" feels too direct and confronting.

Journey link
Discover — "Object has no context; story is locked in memory."

Evidence
Persona Lin Wei: "I want my grandson to know why this watch matters." Supported by tangible-storytelling research [1][3].

MH-2 · Multi-sensory Reveal

Let the object express what words cannot

The system must use multi-sensory reveal (3D + light + sound + motion) to carry the emotional weight elders won't speak aloud and grandchildren find too earnest to verbalize.

Cultural pain
Direct emotional declaration ("I love you", "I miss you") is culturally muted; affection needs a vehicle.

Journey link
Reveal — "Text archives feel dry and unengaging."

Evidence
Persona Xiao Yu: "The pocket watch reveal felt vivid and fun — I actually wanted to ask Grandpa more about it." Story Appeal averages --/5 in our 24-sample survey. Supported by the documented symbolic-and-affective weight of physical mementos [3].

MH-3 · Lightweight Async Reply

Keep each reply short, optional, and asynchronous

The system must allow participation through small, low-pressure fragments (one-tap reaction, short text, voice clip) rather than scheduled calls or long writing sessions.

Cultural pain
Committing to a long phone call feels like an imposition on busy children; both sides avoid it.

Journey link
Reply — "One-way storytelling dies after a single telling."

Evidence
Reply Intent averages --/5 in our 24-sample survey. Supported by collaborative family memory [2] and slow-tech / re-visitation research [4].

How these are validated downstream:

Each must-have maps to one core feature in the System (NFC tap → 3D reveal → async messaging) and one indicator in the Evaluation page (task success at the trigger step, story-appeal rating, reply intent and actual reply rate).

The Gap (Academic Papers)

Four foundational HCI papers in our space — three strengths each (what they contribute) and three misses each (the gap that motivates TimeLens).

[1] Supporting intergenerational memento storytelling for older adults through a tangible display

Li, C., Hu, J., Hengeveld, B. J., & Hummels, C. C. M. · Personal and Ubiquitous Computing 26(3), 625–649 · 2022
3 Strengths
  • Frames memento storytelling as a 4-stage process (trigger → telling → sharing → curating) that maps cleanly onto our system flow
  • Treats young listeners as active agents (memory-trigger providers and "filters"), validating our two-way reply loop
  • Empirically tested with elder–child pairs — provides ground truth on what tangible interaction adds for older users
3 Misses
  • Requires a custom hardware display in the home — not deployable on the smartphones our 3-generation users already own
  • Single-pair use (one elder + one descendant); does not scale to dispersed Chinese families with three generations
  • Story content stays inside the device; no asynchronous, lightweight reply channel for grandchildren away at university
DOI 10.1007/s00779-020-01364-9

[2] Co-constructing Family Memory: Understanding the Intergenerational Practices of Passing on Family Stories

Jones, J. & Ackerman, M. S. · CHI '18 (Honorable Mention) · 2018
3 Strengths
  • Establishes that family storytelling is co-constructive (listeners reshape stories) — grounds our two-way reply philosophy
  • Identifies "asynchronous, asymmetric, socially-weighted" sharing as the core design challenge — exactly the WeChat-group failure mode we target
  • Highlights "interpretive signals" (who, when, why) as critical for understanding shared memory — motivates our story-card structure
3 Misses
  • Empirical interview study only — describes the problem space but does not deliver a working artifact to test against
  • US/Western family sample — does not address East Asian indirect-communication norms or burden-aversion dynamics we document
  • No tangible-trigger angle — focuses on text/archival mediation, leaving the keepsake-as-trigger gap open
DOI 10.1145/3173574.3173998

[3] Family memories in the home: contrasting physical and digital mementos

Petrelli, D. & Whittaker, S. · Personal and Ubiquitous Computing 14, 153–169 · 2010
3 Strengths
  • Empirically establishes that physical mementos hold more emotional weight than digital ones — justifies starting from a physical object
  • Documents the infrequent-access problem of digital mementos — identifies the gap our NFC trigger model directly solves
  • Proposes design guidelines for digital mementos to acquire "symbolic associations" — gives us evaluation criteria for whether the 3D reveal succeeds
3 Misses
  • Predates ubiquitous smartphones, NFC, and AR — its "digital memento" is a 2010-era photo file, not a multi-modal experience
  • Studies UK/EU households — does not address Chinese 3-generation co-living patterns or oral memory traditions
  • Stops at design guidelines — no implementation, no real intergenerational evaluation, no async reply mechanism
DOI 10.1007/s00779-009-0279-7

[4] Designing for Slowness, Anticipation and Re-visitation: A Long Term Field Study of the Photobox

Odom, W., Sellen, A., Banks, R., Kirk, D., Regan, T., Selby, M., Forlizzi, J., & Zimmerman, J. · CHI '14 (Best Paper) · 2014
3 Strengths
  • 14-month field deployment — rare in HCI; gives confidence that "slow" technology can sustain engagement past initial novelty
  • Demonstrates that randomness + delay creates anticipation — the playful affect we want at the moment of NFC reveal
  • Articulates the slow-technology research program with concrete re-visitation metrics we can borrow for evaluation
3 Misses
  • Single-user device — no cross-generation sharing or two-way reply at all
  • Random-print model removes user agency over which memory surfaces; our keepsake-trigger model gives intentional control
  • Not tangible-anchored — photos are summoned by time, not by touching a meaningful object, the playful trigger we need
DOI 10.1145/2556288.2557178
